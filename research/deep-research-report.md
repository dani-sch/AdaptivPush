# Evidence-Based Foundation for Strength-Training Program Design and Adaptive Programming

## Executive summary

### A. Executive summary

The current evidence supports a simple but important hierarchy: the best resistance-training program is not the one with the most complexity, but the one that delivers the right stimulus consistently, matches the trainee’s goal, and can be recovered from. The updated 2026 position stand from the entity["organization","American College of Sports Medicine","professional society in sports medicine and exercise science"] emphasizes that most adults benefit from training all major muscle groups at least twice per week, and that consistency matters more than elaborate splits or “perfect” exercise selection. citeturn1view4turn27search9turn23search13

For strength, the strongest evidence favors heavier loading, longer rests, specific practice of the target lifts or movement patterns, and enough frequency to build skill with those lifts. For hypertrophy, muscle growth occurs across a broad loading spectrum when sets are performed hard enough, but moderate loads are often the most practical, and weekly volume is a major driver of results. Frequency is best understood mostly as a way to distribute volume and improve exercise quality rather than a magic variable by itself. citeturn4search20turn21search21turn21search28turn3search8turn25search32turn27search22

Beginners generally do not need high complexity. They respond well to simple full-body or upper/lower routines, modest volume, repeated practice of basic movement patterns, and small, frequent overloads. Intermediate and advanced lifters usually benefit more from deliberate fatigue management, somewhat higher or more targeted volume, and greater exercise specificity. Direct evidence suggests that periodization has a clearer advantage for maximal strength in trained lifters than for hypertrophy in the average recreational trainee. citeturn27search16turn17search0turn25search31turn4search16

Autoregulation is useful because real-world readiness fluctuates. Sleep loss, high psychosocial stress, mental fatigue, illness, severe soreness, and pain can all reduce performance or recovery, but the practical response is usually to reduce volume first, then exercise complexity, and only then consider reducing load substantially. Subjective well-being measures are often more responsive to training stress than many objective markers, so readiness should be treated as a trend and conversation, not a single “score.” citeturn6search16turn6search20turn7search21turn7search28turn14search1turn24search10turn24search18

Menstrual-cycle-based programming should be optional and individualized. Average phase effects on acute strength performance are mixed, small, or trivial in many reviews, while symptom burden can be very meaningful for individual athletes. The most defensible approach is symptom-informed adjustment based on cramps, fatigue, sleep disruption, mood, motivation, and pain, rather than assuming that all women should train differently by cycle phase. citeturn10search6turn4search11turn10search11turn10search0turn10search3turn10search22

Deloading, stimulus-to-fatigue ratio, minimum effective volume, and maximum recoverable volume are not equal in evidentiary maturity. Minimum effective dose has direct research support; deloads are widely used and reasonable in practice but still under-studied; SFR/MEV/MRV are useful coaching heuristics, yet they should not be treated as fixed physiological thresholds. Wearable readiness metrics and public-facing adaptive workout tools are helpful for trend monitoring, but the research still supports combining those signals with subjective recovery, training performance, and goal-specific programming judgment. citeturn17search0turn17search8turn31search0turn31search8turn31search23turn30search3turn33view6turn33view7turn24search10

## Research foundations and evidence map

### B. Full research report

A useful way to structure resistance training is to think in six interacting layers: stimulus, specificity, recoverability, progression, adherence, and context. The stimulus must be large enough to matter, specific enough to the goal, recoverable enough to repeat, progressed enough to keep adaptation moving, simple enough to sustain, and flexible enough to fit real life. This framing is consistent with the major guideline and review literature, even though different researchers and coaches use different terminology. citeturn1view4turn9search24turn4search20turn30search3

| Individualization | People differ in recovery, skill, injury history, and response | Autoregulation and flexible prescription generally outperform or match rigid loading, especially for strength | Use ranges and targets, not only fixed numbers | Moderate to strong |
| Volume | Weekly hard sets strongly influence hypertrophy | Higher volume generally favors growth up to a point, with diminishing returns and more fatigue | Start moderate, then add only if recovery and performance justify it | Strong |
| Intensity/load | Load affects strength and practicality | Strength favors heavier loading; hypertrophy is possible across a wide loading range if effort is high enough | Use heavier work for strength goals and moderate loads as the practical default for growth | Strong |

Several practical implications follow from these principles. First, for most people, volume and exercise effort matter more than split novelty. Second, exercise order matters because early-session exercises get the best performance; high-skill, high-force, or primary-goal lifts should usually come first. Third, there is no universal need to train to failure; proximity to failure is a tool, not a rule. Fourth, the less experienced the trainee, the less programming complexity is usually needed to progress. citeturn12search20turn3search27turn16search22turn27search16

### E. Best-practice tables for workout splits

At equal weekly volume, split selection is usually a logistics and recoverability decision more than a physiology decision. The 2024 meta-analysis comparing split and full-body routines found no clear advantage of one over the other for strength or hypertrophy when volume was comparable. That means split choice should usually be driven by training frequency, goal emphasis, local fatigue, time per session, and preference. citeturn4search1turn4search17turn4search21

## Exercise selection, muscle targeting, progression, deloading, and session design

### F. Best-practice tables for muscle-group targeting

Exercise selection should reflect function as well as goal. Multi-joint lifts are time-efficient and often excellent base builders, but they do not eliminate the value of single-joint movements, especially when a muscle is being prioritized or when local volume must be increased without large systemic fatigue. Machines, free weights, bands, cables, and bodyweight can all be appropriate; the best choice depends on skill, comfort, available loading, injury history, and how directly the exercise loads the target muscle. citeturn3search17turn3search21turn3search3turn3search7turn21search4turn21search6

| Target | Best exercise categories | Common effective exercises | Practical weekly hard-set starting range | Recommended frequency | Recovery considerations | Common mistakes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Glutes | Hip thrust/bridge, squat/lunge, hinge, abduction | Hip thrust, squat, split squat, RDL, deadlift, step-up, cable abduction | 10–14; 14–20+ when specializing | 2–4x/week | Usually tolerate relatively frequent training if exercise stress is varied | Only short-ROM thrusts, or only kickbacks/abductions |
| Quads | Knee-dominant squat/press, lunge, knee extension | Back squat, front squat, leg press, hack squat, split squat, leg extension | 8–12; 12–18 specialize | 2–3x/week | High soreness if all work is deep eccentric knee-dominant loading | Too little knee flexion depth, no accessory work if growth is the goal |
| Hamstrings | Hip hinge plus knee flexion | RDL, deadlift variant, good morning, leg curl, Nordic variation | 8–12; 10–16 specialize | 2–3x/week | Need both hip-extension and knee-flexion patterns for fuller development | Only hinging, or only curling |
| Calves | Standing and seated plantar flexion, long-length work | Standing calf raise, seated calf raise, donkey calf raise | 10–14; 14–20 specialize | 3–6x/week | Often recover quickly, but full ROM matters | Bouncing, short ROM, never training soleus |
| Chest | Horizontal/incline press and adduction | Bench press, incline press, machine press, push-up, fly/pec-deck | 8–12; 10–18 specialize | 2–3x/week | Shoulder comfort and scapular position matter | Only flat pressing, poor shoulder tolerance |
| Back | Horizontal row, vertical pull, shoulder extension | Row variations, pull-down, pull-up, chest-supported row, straight-arm pull-down | 10–14; 14–22 specialize | 2–4x/week | “Back” volume spans lats, upper back, spinal erectors | Counting only vertical pulls as full back work |
| Shoulders | Overhead press, lateral raise, rear-delt work | OHP, machine press, lateral raise, reverse fly, cable Y-raise | 8–12 direct; 10–16 specialize | 2–4x/week | Front delts already get pressing work; lateral and rear delts often need direct work | Overemphasizing front delts |
| Biceps | Elbow flexion with different shoulder positions/grips | Curl variations, incline curl, preacher curl, cable curl | 6–10 direct; 8–14 specialize | 2–4x/week | Often recover quickly, but elbows can get irritated if variation is poor | Relying only on rows and pull-downs |
| Triceps | Pressing plus elbow extension, especially overhead | Pressing, cable press-down, skullcrusher, overhead extension | 6–10 direct; 8–14 specialize | 2–4x/week | Long-head development benefits from overhead work | Only press-downs, no overhead extension |
| Core | Anti-extension, anti-rotation, lateral stability, trunk flexion as needed | Plank variations, rollout, pallof press, side plank, cable crunch | 4–10 direct; 6–12 specialize | 2–5x/week | Low fatigue cost allows frequent dosing | Only doing planks forever, no progression |
| Full lower body | Balance knee-dominant, hip-dominant, calf work | Squat/press + hinge + unilateral + calves | 12–20 total across patterns | 2–4x/week | Manage local soreness so frequency stays viable | All hinge or all squat, no unilateral work |
| Full upper body | Balance horizontal/vertical push and pull, plus arms/delts as needed | Press + row + pull-down/pull-up + delts/arms | 12–20 total across patterns | 2–4x/week | Shoulder and elbow tolerance steer exercise choice | Too much pressing relative to pulling |

Sources for this table: citeturn31search22turn25search32turn13search0turn13search32turn13search6turn13search14turn13search9turn13search11turn3search17turn3search3

Exercise order is one of the clearer “small variables” that matters in practice. Put high-skill, high-force, or high-priority exercises first, then secondary compounds, then accessories, then isolation work, then optional conditioning. If a muscle group is the clear priority, train it earlier in the session or earlier in the week. Supersets can save time and can be effective, but they should usually pair noncompeting exercises when strength performance is important. citeturn12search20turn0search13turn25search19

Exercise variation deserves restraint. The literature supports some variation for motivation and potentially regional adaptation, but not random churn. Primary compounds used to track progress usually deserve longer continuity, often roughly 6–12 weeks or longer unless pain, equipment limits, or clear stagnation justify change. Accessories can rotate more often, often every 2–6 weeks if desired. Those timelines are more coaching practice than hard science; what the evidence clearly supports is that excessive variation makes progression harder to evaluate. citeturn25search1turn25search12

### G. Progression and deload research summary

| Progression method | Best for | How it works | Pros | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Linear progression | Beginners | Add small load increases frequently when targets are achieved | Simple, motivating, effective early | Stalls quickly in trained lifters |
| Double progression | Hypertrophy, general fitness | Hold a rep range; add reps first, then add load once top of range is reached | Practical, self-limiting, easy to coach | Slower load progression for pure strength |
| Percentage-based progression | Specific strength goals | Set loads from a tested or estimated max | Useful for lift-specific planning and peaking | Assumes stable daily readiness |
| RPE-based progression | Intermediate/advanced strength | Prescribe sets by perceived effort target | Adjusts load to daily performance | Needs experience and calibration |
| RIR-based progression | Hypertrophy and mixed goals | Train to a target number of reps left “in reserve” | Good for fatigue control and volume quality | Novices often misjudge RIR initially |
| Volume progression | Hypertrophy specialization | Add hard sets over time | Powerful when recovery is good | Easy way to create junk fatigue |
| Rep progression | General strength/hypertrophy | Keep load steady and increase reps | Good when smallest load jump is too large | Can become endurance-biased if overused |
| Set progression | Intermediates with good recovery | Add a set at same load/rep target | Raises weekly stimulus without constant heavier loading | Time cost rises quickly |

Sources for this table: citeturn27search1turn25search4turn25search15turn16search17turn16search8turn4search6turn16search22

Safe load increases are usually best kept small. The classic ACSM progression guidance recommends increasing load by roughly 2–10% once the trainee can exceed the desired reps by one or two repetitions across repeated sessions. In practice, smaller jumps are usually better for upper-body or isolation lifts, while somewhat larger jumps are more tolerable on larger lower-body compounds. If the next load jump is too large, rep progression is often a better choice than forcing the weight increase. citeturn27search1turn27search16

Plateaus should be treated as a diagnostic question, not just a willpower problem. A plateau is more meaningful when performance fails to improve for several weeks despite good adherence, stable technique, adequate protein and energy intake, and reasonable recovery. The first response should usually be to audit the basics: missed sessions, rushed rests, poor sleep, life stress, body-mass changes, inappropriate exercise variation, or simply too much accumulated fatigue. Physiologically, gains in hypertrophy and strength slow as training age rises, so “slower progress” is normal and not always a true plateau. citeturn17search2turn30search3turn24search10turn31search15

A deload is a short, intentional reduction in training stress to dissipate fatigue while preserving the habit and most of the adaptation. The research base for deloading is still small. Recent studies show that planned short deloads do not automatically improve outcomes compared with continuous training, and may slightly reduce short-term strength progress if inserted unnecessarily. On the other hand, survey and coaching data show deloading is widely used to reduce fatigue, transition between blocks, or prepare for performance changes. The most defensible conclusion is that deloads are often reasonable, but they should be used purposefully rather than automatically. citeturn31search0turn31search8turn31search23

| Deload type | When it fits best | Common structure | What the evidence suggests |
|---|---|---|---|
| Reactive deload | Unexpected fatigue, under-recovery, repeated performance drop, high life stress | Reduce volume by ~30–70%; maintain some load; often keep frequency similar or slightly lower | Most justifiable in practice |
| Scheduled deload | Advanced training blocks, peaking cycles, highly fatiguing specialization phases | 1 week every several hard weeks, usually with substantial volume drop | Can help manage fatigue, but is not always superior if the trainee was coping fine |
| Taper for strength sport | Before meets or testing | Volume down, specificity maintained, heavy exposures preserved but fewer | Supported better than generic deloading |
| “Complete rest week” | Illness, acute overload, travel, or necessity | Temporary cessation | May be acceptable when needed, but routine stoppage is not clearly beneficial |

Rest periods should be long enough to preserve output at the priority exercises. Low-readiness sessions should usually reduce volume and exercise complexity more than they reduce movement practice altogether. citeturn12search20turn27search22turn0search13turn12search22

## Personalization, readiness, menstrual cycle, and recovery

### H. Readiness and recovery research summary

Personalization should not be confused with endless complexity. Most individualization is accomplished by adjusting four things: total weekly volume, exercise choice, proximity to failure, and session frequency. Many factors matter, but not every factor requires a separate rule. citeturn16search22turn30search3turn24search10

| Factor | What it can affect | Evidence strength | Practical adjustment | When no adjustment is necessary |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Age | Recovery rate, joint tolerance, power loss, sarcopenia risk | Strong | Start conservatively, increase gradually, include strength and power intent where appropriate | Older adults do not automatically need only light loads |
| Biological sex | Absolute strength/body composition; not necessarily relative trainability | Moderate to strong | Do not default to sex-specific programming; adjust by individual response and symptoms | Standard strength/hypertrophy principles still apply |
| Training experience | Volume tolerance, need for specificity, rate of progress | Strong | Beginners: simpler, lower volume; advanced: more specificity and finer fatigue management | None |
| Body size / current strength | Absolute loading, technical demands | Moderate | Use relative effort and movement comfort, not stereotypes | Body weight alone should not dictate split choice |
| Injury history | Exercise tolerance, ROM, stability needs | Strong for symptom-guided exercise therapy | Favor stable, symptom-tolerated options and progress slowly | If fully asymptomatic and adapted, no special rule may be needed |
| Recovery capacity | How much volume/frequency is sustainable | Moderate | Adjust volume before changing everything else | None |
| Available time | Session design and split feasibility | Strong | Use full-body, supersets, efficient exercise selection | If time is abundant, no special simplification is needed |
| Equipment | Exercise options and loading precision | Strong | Substitute by movement pattern and resistance profile | If equipment is abundant, choose the simplest effective option |
| Sleep quality and duration | Readiness, power, perceived exertion, recovery | Strong | Reduce volume or difficulty on bad-sleep days if performance is clearly suppressed | One mildly poor night does not always require large change |
| Stress and mental fatigue | Recovery, strength endurance, motivation | Moderate to strong | Volume and density reductions are usually more appropriate than eliminating training | Low stress needs no special adjustment |
| Soreness and fatigue | Local readiness and movement quality | Strong for acute impact | Mild DOMS can be trained through; severe soreness merits local reduction | Mild symmetrical soreness alone is not a reason to cancel |
| Menstrual symptoms if tracked | Comfort, sleep, energy, motivation, pain | Moderate but highly individual | Use symptom-informed adjustments, not automatic phase rules | If asymptomatic, no adjustment is necessary |

The readiness evidence supports a practical triage model. If readiness is modestly reduced, it is usually still fine to train, but the session should be simplified. Poor recovery most often justifies reducing volume, not skipping all movement. Skipping training becomes more appropriate when illness is systemic, pain is abnormal, or performance is clearly deteriorating across multiple readiness markers. citeturn14search1turn14search3turn8search5turn8search10

| State | Practical response |
|---|---|
| Slept well, normal soreness, low stress | Train normally |
| One poor night of sleep, mild DOMS, normal motivation | Usually train normally or trim one accessory set |
| Repeated poor sleep, high stress, mental fatigue, performance feels unusually flat | Keep the main lift, reduce total sets by ~20–40%, avoid failure and very fatiguing accessories |
| Severe local soreness that limits ROM or technique | Train a different region or use a lighter, shorter local session |
| Mild head-cold symptoms without chest symptoms or fever | Conservative judgment; reduce intensity or rest if symptoms worsen |
| Fever, chest symptoms, dizziness, major body aches, or unusual weakness | Rest and avoid hard training |
| Sharp, localized, worsening, or asymmetric pain; swelling; bruising; neurological symptoms; major functional loss | Stop the session and seek professional evaluation |

For nutrition and recovery, the strongest evidence supports total daily protein sufficiency more than obsession with narrow “anabolic windows.” Protein supplementation on top of resistance training adds small but meaningful gains in lean body mass on average, and higher protein intakes are often especially useful during energy restriction. Hydration matters more in hot conditions and with larger sweat losses than in every ordinary gym session; marked hypohydration is more consistently harmful than small day-to-day shifts. citeturn23search8turn23search15turn23search23turn23search20turn23search2turn23search26

Active recovery, walking, and low-intensity cardio are generally reasonable on rest days when they improve how the trainee feels without adding meaningful fatigue. Foam rolling can slightly improve ROM and may modestly help soreness or short-term performance recovery. Post-exercise stretching, used by itself, does not appear to meaningfully improve soreness or strength recovery. Regular cold-water immersion immediately after lifting may reduce soreness perception, but repeated use can attenuate hypertrophy and strength adaptation, so it is usually a poor default for muscle-growth phases. citeturn32search2turn32search6turn32search7turn32search30turn32search4turn32search20

### I. Menstrual-cycle training research summary

The most careful current conclusion is that menstrual-cycle phase alone does not justify universal program changes. Reviews and consensus statements consistently highlight methodological problems in past research, small average effects, and substantial between-person variability. Individual symptom tracking is more actionable than phase-based assumptions. citeturn10search6turn4search11turn10search11turn10search3

| Topic | What the evidence suggests | Practical interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Menstrual phase | Some women report worse symptoms and feel less ready; average strength effects are not consistently large | Adjust if cramps, fatigue, sleep disruption, or pain are meaningful; otherwise normal training may be fine |
| Follicular phase | Often assumed to be a universally “better” phase, but evidence is not strong enough for broad claims | Do not automatically add volume or intensity just because of the calendar |
| Ovulation | Average performance advantages are inconsistent across studies | No universal programming rule |
| Luteal phase / PMS | Symptoms such as bloating, sleep disruption, low mood, or perceived fatigue may impair session quality in some individuals | Volume or exercise simplification may help when symptoms are high |
| Hormonal contraception | Evidence does not support a simple universal conclusion that contraception impairs adaptation | Ask what symptoms or patterns actually occur rather than assuming |
| Irregular cycles | Calendar-based predictions become less useful | Symptom-informed decisions become even more important |
| Menstrual tracking in training | Helpful when voluntary, private, and autonomy-supportive | Use to detect patterns, not to stereotype or overcontrol |

### J. Mobility, warm-up, cooldown, and injury-prevention summary

Warm-ups have a clearer evidence base for performance preparation than cooldowns do for recovery. A practical warm-up usually includes a short general phase, dynamic and movement-specific preparation, and specific ramp-up sets for the main lift. Dynamic stretching tends to support performance better than static stretching immediately before explosive work, while chronic resistance training itself can improve range of motion. citeturn8search6turn6search33turn12search24turn12search30

| Component | Practical evidence-based use |
|---|---|
| General warm-up | 5–10 minutes of easy cyclical movement or brisk walking to raise temperature |
| Specific warm-up sets | Several progressively heavier sets before compound lifts |
| Dynamic mobility | Use where ROM is restricted for the planned task, not as random filler |
| Activation drills | Low-fatigue primers can help cue positions, but they are not magical |
| Cooldown | Optional; useful if it helps breathing, transition, or comfort, but not required for adaptation |
| Stretching | Better for ROM than for immediate post-lift recovery; chronic use can improve flexibility |
| Light rehab/prehab work | Most useful when linked to an actual limitation or recurrent issue |

Sources for this table: citeturn8search6turn6search2turn6search33turn12search3turn12search24turn32search7

Practical examples that fit the evidence are straightforward. A lower-body warm-up can include 3–5 minutes of light cycling, bodyweight squats, hip hinges, split-squat patterning, ankle rocks, and then ramp-up squat or hinge sets. An upper-body warm-up can include light rowing or arm cycling, scapular control work, pressing and pulling pattern rehearsal, and ramp-up sets for the first major press or row. A full-body warm-up simply combines those elements with brief movement-specific rehearsal instead of a long isolated mobility circuit. citeturn8search6turn12search20turn6search33

Mobility work is most helpful when it addresses a real ROM bottleneck. Hip mobility may help some squat or hinge patterns when hip flexion, extension, or rotation is limited. Ankle dorsiflexion can affect squat mechanics in some people. Shoulder prep matters most when the trainee lacks tolerance or control in overhead or pressing motions. For persistent low-back pain or recurrent shoulder/knee symptoms, the safer evidence-based principle is to keep training symptom-tolerated and progressively loaded, not necessarily to stop resistance training altogether. Exercise-based rehabilitation remains a core recommendation in guidelines for musculoskeletal pain conditions such as persistent back pain and osteoarthritis. citeturn15search22turn15search34turn15search16turn15search21turn15search23

## Safety, context, limitations, and references

### K. Research limitations and open questions

The most important safety point is that normal training discomfort is not the same as injury pain. Hard effort, burning, muscular fatigue, and mild symmetrical soreness are common and not inherently harmful. Red flags are pain that is sharp, localized, worsening, mechanically unstable, associated with bruising or swelling, accompanied by numbness or weakness, or severe enough to alter normal movement or daily function. Training advice should therefore preserve user autonomy and avoid “no pain, no gain” framing, especially when dealing with pain, menstrual symptoms, or body-composition goals. Autonomy-supportive and preference-sensitive approaches are more compatible with long-term adherence than shame-based approaches. citeturn8search5turn8search10turn15search16turn22search18turn22search25

The current public landscape of training tools reflects some of the evidence but not all of it. urlFitbodturn18search6 publicly emphasizes personalized workouts that adapt to the user’s body, recovery, and progress. urlStrongturn18search0 and urlHevyturn18search1 publicly center workout logging, progress tracking, and routines. urlBoostcampturn18search2 emphasizes programs, analytics, RPE tracking, and auto-progression. urlABC Trainerizeturn19search0 centers coach-delivered programming and integrations, and its help documentation now includes an AI workout builder. urlApple Healthturn20search7 and Apple Watch training-load features compare 7-day and 28-day workload and also allow cycle, symptom, and sleep logging. urlWHOOPturn19search2 and urlGarmin Training Readinessturn19search21 foreground readiness or recovery metrics built from sleep, strain/load, HRV, and stress history. citeturn33view0turn33view1turn33view2turn33view3turn33view4turn33view5turn33view6turn33view7turn19search2

What current tools appear to capture well, at least from public-facing materials, is workout logging, broad progressive overload, routine delivery, and trend-based workload or recovery signals. What appears less fully developed in public product descriptions is nuanced management of local soreness versus pain, structured deload decisions, symptom-informed menstrual adjustments, the known limits of RPE/RIR accuracy in novices, and strength-specific fatigue management that prioritizes volume reduction before wholesale session cancellation. That gap is aligned with the literature, which suggests that subjective readiness is important but not perfectly reducible to a single score, and that many programming decisions still depend on context and coaching judgment. This is an inference from product documentation plus the exercise-science literature, not a claim that any one product cannot do these things in practice. citeturn33view0turn33view1turn33view2turn33view3turn33view4turn33view6turn33view7turn24search10turn24search18turn16search8turn31search23

The largest open questions remain the ones that practitioners care about most. Exact volume thresholds are still highly individual. Deloading has much less direct evidence than its popularity would suggest. Muscle-specific specialization research is stronger for some regions than others. Menstrual-cycle research is improving but still limited by phase verification, symptom diversity, and underrepresentation of ecologically valid training designs. Readiness wearables and HRV-based systems are promising as monitoring tools, but they still are not validated substitutes for performance data, lived symptom reports, and context-aware load management in resistance training. citeturn31search22turn31search0turn31search8turn13search0turn10search3turn24search0turn24search10
